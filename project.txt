(a) State of the Art and Relevant Literature
Deep learning has become a dominant paradigm in medical imaging analysis, with significant impact in
oncology. Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) have been extensively used for tumor detection,
segmentation, and classification across diverse modalities such as CT, MRI, and histopathology images.
Large public datasets, including The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA), BraTS, and LIDC-IDRI, have played a
critical role in advancing data-driven cancer imaging research by enabling reproducible benchmarking
and model comparison [1–3].
More recently, Vision Transformers (ViTs) have emerged as a powerful alternative to CNNs by modeling
long-range spatial dependencies through self-attention mechanisms. Originally proposed by Dosovitskiy
et al. [4], ViTs have been successfully adapted for medical imaging tasks, demonstrating strong
performance in tumor segmentation and classification due to their ability to capture global context

[5,6]. Hierarchical transformer architectures such as Swin Transformer further improve scalability and
have been applied to medical image analysis, including cancer imaging [7].
Despite these advances, most cancer-related AI models operate on static images, focusing on single
time-point analysis. Cancer, however, is a progressive disease whose behavior depends on time,
biological processes, and treatment interventions. Some studies have explored longitudinal modeling
using recurrent neural networks or temporal CNNs; however, these approaches typically output
numerical predictions such as survival probabilities or growth rates, which are often difficult for
clinicians and patients to interpret visually [8].
Parallel to progress in vision modeling, Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated strong
capabilities in natural language understanding, reasoning, and multimodal integration. In the medical
domain, LLMs have been applied to clinical text summarization, radiology report generation, and
question answering, showing promise in supporting clinical reasoning [9–11]. Recent works highlight the
ability of LLMs to combine medical knowledge with structured inputs; however, their integration with
medical imaging features remains limited and is often restricted to generating textual reports rather
than actionable multimodal reasoning [12].
Another rapidly evolving area is generative modeling, particularly diffusion-based models for image and
video synthesis. Diffusion models have achieved state-of-the-art results in generating high-quality
images and videos in natural domains [13,14]. In medical imaging, generative models have primarily
been explored for data augmentation, reconstruction, and privacy-preserving synthetic data generation
[15,16]. While these applications are valuable, they generally do not address disease progression or
clinical interpretability.
At present, there is limited research combining vision-based representations, language-based clinical
reasoning, and generative video modeling to explicitly visualize disease evolution over time. In oncology,
no established method exists that can generate interpretable, temporally coherent visualizations of
tumor progression conditioned on different treatment strategies, leaving a significant gap between
advanced AI modeling and real clinical needs.
(b) Research Problem, Knowledge Gaps, and Research Questions
The primary research problem addressed by this project is the lack of interpretable and dynamic AI
models for longitudinal cancer progression analysis. While current deep learning systems can achieve
high predictive accuracy, they suffer from three critical limitations.
First, most existing approaches rely on static image analysis, which does not adequately reflect the
temporal nature of cancer development or response to treatment. Second, predictions are typically
expressed as abstract numerical outputs (e.g., probabilities or risk scores), offering limited
interpretability and minimal support for clinical decision-making or patient communication. Third,
existing models do not support counterfactual reasoning, an essential component of personalized
medicine that allows clinicians to explore alternative treatment outcomes.
These gaps motivate the following research questions:
 Can Vision Transformer-based models capture meaningful representations of tumor morphology
and its evolution from longitudinal medical imaging?
 Can Large Language Models integrate imaging-derived features with clinical metadata to
provide coherent, medically grounded reasoning about disease progression?

Overall Objective
The primary objective is to design, implement, and evaluate a multimodal artificial intelligence framework that
integrates Large Vision Models (LVMs), Vision Transformers (ViTs), and Large Language Models (LLMs) to analyze,
explain, and simulate cancer progression from longitudinal medical imaging data.
The project aims to move beyond conventional static cancer image analysis by enabling temporal modeling,
multimodal reasoning, and explainable clinical insights, with a particular emphasis on interpretability and
generative modeling of disease trajectories.
This project is part of a broader research initiative led by Dr. Belkacem Chikhaoui at TELUQ university on
multimodal AI for medical decision support and explainable clinical intelligence, targeting applications in oncology
and precision medicine. The outcomes of this internship are expected to contribute to a peer-reviewed
publication, as well as to serve as a foundation for future MSc/PhD theses and collaborative projects in medical AI.
Specific Objectives and Sub-Objectives
The project is structured around five tightly connected objectives, each associated with concrete technical tasks
and learning outcomes for the intern.
Objective 1 – Develop a Robust Pipeline for Longitudinal Cancer Imaging Analysis
Goal:
To build a reproducible and clinically meaningful data pipeline for processing and organizing longitudinal cancer
imaging data (MRI or CT).
Sub-objectives:
 Acquire and curate publicly available oncology datasets (e.g., TCGA, BraTS, LIDC-IDRI).
 Perform standardized preprocessing, including normalization, spatial alignment, and temporal consistency
checks across patient visits.
 Structure patient data into longitudinal image sequences suitable for temporal and generative modeling.
The intern will implement preprocessing pipelines using Python-based medical imaging libraries (e.g., MONAI,
SimpleITK), validate consistency across time points, and systematically document data quality and limitations.
Objective 2 – Learn High-Level Tumor Representations Using Vision Transformers

Goal:
To extract semantically rich and temporally informative representations of tumors using state-of-the-art Vision
Transformer architectures.
Sub-objectives:
 Implement and fine-tune Vision Transformer models (e.g., ViT, Swin Transformer) on cancer imaging data.
 Compare Transformer-based representations with CNN baselines.
 Learn embeddings that capture tumor morphology, spatial heterogeneity, and temporal evolution.
The intern will train transformer-based models using longitudinal imaging snapshots, analyze embedding evolution
across time, and evaluate representation quality using downstream prediction and clustering tasks.
Objective 3 – Integrate Imaging and Clinical Context Using Large Language Models
Goal:
To enable multimodal clinical reasoning by combining visual tumor representations with structured clinical
metadata using LLMs.
Sub-objectives:
 Design multimodal representations that fuse imaging embeddings with clinical variables (e.g., cancer
stage, treatment type).
 Develop prompt-based strategies to guide LLMs in generating clinically coherent explanations of disease
progression.
 Align visual evidence with textual medical reasoning for interpretability.
The intern will experiment with LLM prompting and embedding alignment techniques, generating explanatory text
linked explicitly to temporal imaging changes.
Objective 4 – Generate and Analyze Cancer Progression Videos
Goal:
To model and visualize cancer progression through AI-generated video sequences conditioned on multimodal
inputs.
Sub-objectives:
 Adapt diffusion-based or transformer-based video generation models to medical imaging data.
 Generate temporally consistent disease progression videos.
 Explore counterfactual scenarios (e.g., treatment vs. no treatment trajectories).
The intern will condition generative models on vision and language embeddings, evaluate temporal coherence,
and analyze the clinical plausibility of generated trajectories.
Objective 5 – Evaluate Explainability, Clinical Plausibility, and Scientific Impact
Goal:
To rigorously assess the performance, interpretability, and medical relevance of the proposed multimodal
framework.
Sub-objectives:
 Quantitatively evaluate model performance and temporal consistency.
 Qualitatively assess explainability through clinician-aligned interpretations.
 Prepare a comprehensive technical report and a publication-ready manuscript draft.
The intern will apply quantitative metrics, conduct qualitative analysis of explanations and videos, and synthesize
results into structured scientific documentation.
